AI Vocabulary Cheat Sheet for Salespeople (Fast Lane US)

This cheat sheet provides simple definitions and common use cases for key AI terms. It emphasizes Generative AI (a hot topic in AI training) while also covering foundational and adjacent concepts. Use it to quickly grasp terms and examples when discussing AI training courses with clients of varying technical backgrounds.

Generative AI & Related Terms

Term Simple Definition Common Use Cases
Generative AI A form of AI that can produce new content (text, images, audio, etc.) based on the data it was trained on (What is Generative AI? | Examples, Use Cases | SAP). In other words, it “creates” original outputs rather than just analyzing existing data. Writing articles or marketing copy; generating images or designs from text prompts; coding assistants that generate programming code; conversational chatbots that produce human-like responses.
Foundation Model A large-scale AI model trained on a broad dataset that can be adapted and fine-tuned for many different tasks (Foundation Models Explained). It serves as a general-purpose base (or "foundation") for multiple applications (e.g. GPT-4 or DALL-E 2 are foundation models). Adapting a pre-trained model to a specific business use-case (like fine-tuning a language model for customer service); leveraging one model for various tasks (summarization, translation, etc.) to save development time.
Large Language Model (LLM) A type of AI model (usually based on deep learning) trained on massive amounts of text, enabling it to understand and generate human-like language (What Are LLMs? | NVIDIA Blog). LLMs predict text one word at a time, producing coherent sentences and paragraphs. Chatbots and virtual assistants that answer questions in natural language; generating reports or emails automatically; language translation services; summarizing long documents; writing code or formulas from descriptions.
Transformer A deep learning model architecture introduced by Google in 2017, built on a self-attention mechanism that allows the model to weigh different parts of the input in parallel (Transformer Architecture - Wikipedia). Transformers enabled large models to capture context more effectively, and they power modern language AI like GPT. Underpins many AI applications (not a user-facing app itself). For example, used in language translation tools, text summarization, and content generation. Any advanced NLP task today likely uses a Transformer-based model for better accuracy and speed.
Generative Pre-trained Transformer (GPT) A family of advanced language models (developed by OpenAI) based on the Transformer architecture (What is GPT? | IBM). “Pre-trained” means they learn from huge text datasets first, then can be fine-tuned for specific tasks. GPT models (like GPT-3 or GPT-4) can generate remarkably human-like text and are the technology behind ChatGPT. Powering AI chatbots (e.g. ChatGPT) that converse with users; content creation tools (writing articles, social media posts); code generation assistants (writing or debugging code); answering questions and knowledge base search (by providing natural language answers).
Prompt Engineering The process of crafting and refining the input prompts (questions or instructions) given to a generative AI model to get the best possible result (Prompt Engineering | Georgia Tech). It’s essentially learning how to “ask” the AI in the right way—often described as “coding in English.” Optimizing chatbot questions to yield more accurate answers; creating step-by-step prompts for AI to generate a desired image or text (e.g. telling an image AI the style, subject, and details); developing reusable prompt templates for content generation (marketing emails, code comments, etc.).
Chatbot (AI Chatbot) An AI program designed to simulate conversation with human users in natural language (Chatbot vs Virtual Assistant | nasscom). Modern chatbots often use LLMs to generate responses, making them far more fluid and “human-sounding” than old rule-based bots. Customer service bots handling FAQs and support requests; virtual assistants on websites or messaging apps that help with tasks (booking appointments, tracking orders); internal helpdesk bots answering employees’ IT or HR questions; voice assistants (when paired with speech-to-text) like Alexa or Siri.
Diffusion Model A type of generative AI model that creates high-quality outputs through a structured denoising process (Definition of Diffusion Models - Time). It starts with random noise and iteratively refines it into a coherent image or data sample (essentially learning to reverse the process of adding noise). AI image generation (e.g. tools like Stable Diffusion that create art or photorealistic images from text descriptions); image editing/fill (inpainting or modifying parts of an image by treating the edit as a generative task); generating audio (like realistic music or voices) by a similar noise-refinement approach.
Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) A generative model architecture with two neural networks “adversarially” training one another (What is GenAI? | TechTarget). One network (the generator) tries to create fake data (e.g. an image) that looks real, while the second (the discriminator) tries to detect fakes. They continually improve together, resulting in very realistic generated data over time. Creating realistic synthetic images or videos (e.g. generating faces of people who don’t exist, or “deepfakes” swapping someone’s face in a video); generating art or design prototypes; producing synthetic training data for AI (when real data is limited or sensitive); upscaling or enhancing images (generator produces high-resolution details).

Foundational & Adjacent AI Terms

Term Simple Definition Common Use Cases
Artificial Intelligence (AI) The broad field of computer science focused on creating machines or software that can perform tasks requiring human intelligence (Guide to AI | Phrase). In practice, AI refers to any technique that enables computers to mimic cognitive functions like learning from data, recognizing patterns, understanding language, or making decisions. Virtually any domain: Automation of routine tasks (e.g. AI scheduling meetings or routing customer tickets), data analysis (finding trends or anomalies in big data), image and speech recognition (face unlock on phones, voice assistants), and decision support (AI-powered fraud detection, medical diagnosis aids).
Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) A hypothetical future AI that possesses broad, human-like intelligence across a wide range of tasks (What is Generative AI? | SAP). Unlike today’s “narrow AI” (which excels at specific tasks), AGI would be able to understand, learn, and apply knowledge to any problem, much like a human. (Note: AGI does not exist yet with current technology.) Currently no real-world use cases (AGI is an aspirational concept). In theory, an AGI could handle almost any job or problem – from scientific research to common-sense reasoning – but today’s discussions around AGI are largely academic or speculative.
Machine Learning (ML) A subset of AI that focuses on algorithms allowing computers to learn from data and improve over time, without being explicitly programmed for each scenario (Understanding AI, ML, Data Science | ClicData). Essentially, the system finds patterns in historical data and uses those patterns to make predictions or decisions on new data. Predictive analytics (forecasting sales, demand, or maintenance needs from past data); recommendation engines (suggesting products or content based on user behavior); classification tasks (spam email detection, image recognition of objects or defects); any scenario where the system can “learn” rules from examples (fraud detection from transaction history, medical diagnosis from patient data).
Supervised Learning An ML approach where the model is trained on labeled data (input examples each tagged with the correct output) (What is Supervised ML? | HPE). The model learns by comparing its predictions to the known answers and adjusting until it can predict accurately. This is like learning with a teacher, since the “right answers” are provided. Classification (e.g., training an email filter with emails labeled “spam” or “not spam” so it can classify future emails) and regression (e.g., learning to predict a continuous value like housing prices from historical labeled data). Also used in training image recognition (labels = object names in images) and speech recognition (labels = transcribed words).
Unsupervised Learning An ML approach where the model is given unlabeled data and must find patterns or groupings on its own (What is Unsupervised Learning? | PromptLayer). There are no explicit “right answers”; the algorithm tries to discover the data’s structure (e.g. clustering similar items or detecting anomalies) without guidance. Customer segmentation (grouping customers into clusters based on purchasing behavior without pre-defined categories); anomaly detection (finding unusual transactions that could indicate fraud, without having examples of fraud labeled); data compression or pattern discovery (reducing dimensionality of data, finding latent features in datasets). This is useful when you have lots of data but no existing labels or categories.
Reinforcement Learning (RL) An ML paradigm where an agent learns by interacting with an environment and getting feedback in the form of rewards or penalties (Reinforcement Learning | Fiveable). The agent tries different actions and is “reinforced” to repeat actions that yield positive rewards. Over time, it learns an optimal strategy (policy) to maximize reward. Training game AIs (a classic example is AlphaGo, which learned to play Go by trial and error with reward for winning); robotics and automation (a robot learns to navigate a maze or balance a device by getting reward for progress); dynamic decision systems (e.g. an RL-based system that adjusts pricing or resource allocation based on reward outcomes like profit or efficiency).
Neural Network A type of model inspired by the human brain, consisting of layers of interconnected nodes (“neurons”) that collectively learn to recognize patterns in data (What is a Neural Network? | HackerRank). Each neuron performs a simple computation, but together the network can model complex relationships. Deeper (many-layered) neural networks are the backbone of most modern AI breakthroughs. Underlying technology for many AI applications: image recognition (networks detect features like edges, shapes, then objects in pictures), speech recognition (converting audio waveforms to text), natural language processing (understanding text meaning). Essentially, whenever you hear “deep learning,” it’s using neural networks – from autonomous vehicle vision to recommendation systems’ ranking algorithms.
Deep Learning A subset of machine learning that uses multi-layered neural networks to learn complex patterns from large amounts of data (What Is Deep Learning? | IBM). “Deep” refers to having many layers in the neural network, which enables learning very abstract, high-level features. Deep learning has driven major advances in AI by handling tasks that were previously hard-coded. Computer vision (deep learning models achieving human-like accuracy in identifying faces, animals, tumors in X-rays, etc.); natural language understanding (powering translators and chatbots); speech-to-text and text-to-speech (virtual assistants, dictation software). Deep learning is also used in recommendation systems, generative models, and anywhere a complex function mapping is needed from data.
Natural Language Processing (NLP) A branch of AI that enables computers to understand, interpret, and generate human language (text or speech) (Introduction to NLP | LinkedIn). NLP encompasses a range of language tasks – from simply parsing sentences to understanding context and sentiment. It combines linguistics with machine learning to allow interaction with computers in our language. Language translation (e.g. Google Translate uses NLP to convert text between languages); sentiment analysis (analyzing social media or reviews to determine if text is positive or negative); chatbots and voice assistants (understanding user queries and generating responses); information extraction (reading documents to pull out key facts like names, dates, events).
Computer Vision (CV) The field of AI focused on enabling machines to interpret and understand visual information from the world (images or video) (What is Computer Vision? | Moveworks). It involves teaching computers to recognize objects, people, scenes, and activities in visual data. Modern CV often uses deep learning to identify patterns in pixels. Image recognition and classification (tagging objects or people in photos, as used in social media or security cameras); object detection and tracking (for self-driving cars to recognize pedestrians and lanes, or retail AI to track products on shelves); medical imaging (detecting tumors, analyzing X-rays/MRI for abnormalities); facial recognition (security or unlocking phones); quality inspection in manufacturing (spotting defects via camera).
Recommendation System An AI-driven system that suggests relevant items to users (products, content, etc.) based on data about their preferences or behavior (Recommendation System | LinkedIn). It filters information and predicts what the user would like, typically using past user activity and sometimes similarity to other users/items. E-commerce product recommendations (“You might also like...” on Amazon, based on your browsing and purchase history); streaming services recommending movies or songs (Netflix, Spotify suggestions tailored to your tastes); social media feeds (showing posts or ads you’re likely to engage with); any personalized experience where the system learns what a user might want next.
Data Science An interdisciplinary field that combines statistics, programming, and domain expertise to extract insights and knowledge from data (Data Science Definition | Patsnap). Data science involves collecting and cleaning data, analyzing it (often with ML algorithms), and interpreting the results to inform decisions. (It’s closely related to AI: many data scientists use ML as a tool.) Business analytics (finding trends in sales data, customer demographics, etc. to guide strategy); predictive modeling (forecasting market trends or risk using statistical models); experiment analysis (A/B testing results, scientific data analysis); building dashboards and reports for data-driven decision making. In essence, whenever an organization leverages large data sets to gain competitive insight (from customer behavior to operational efficiency), that’s data science at work.
MLOps (Machine Learning Operations) A set of practices and tools for deploying, monitoring, and maintaining ML models in production in a reliable and efficient way (The confusion about MLOps | Datumo). MLOps extends DevOps principles to ML, ensuring that models keep working as expected when integrated into real-world applications. It covers versioning of models, testing, continuous integration, and ongoing performance tracking. Deploying a trained model as a service (e.g. an API for an AI-driven feature) and continually updating it as new data arrives; monitoring an online recommendation model to detect “drift” if user behavior changes and retraining it as needed; automating data pipeline and model retraining for a predictive maintenance system. Essentially, any scenario where an ML model moves from a data scientist’s notebook to a live product (and needs to stay accurate over time) requires MLOps practices.
No-Code AI AI development platforms or tools that allow users to create models and AI-driven applications without writing code. They provide intuitive interfaces (drag-and-drop or visual tools) so non-programmers can build or utilize AI solutions (No-code AI | LeewayHertz). No-Code AI “democratizes” AI by lowering the technical barrier. A business analyst using a GUI to train a simple predictive model on sales data (instead of coding in Python); building a chatbot by configuring dialogue flows in a dashboard; using AutoML services where you upload labeled data and the tool automatically trains and tunes a model; creating an image classification model by just providing examples through a web interface. These let domain experts prototype AI solutions quickly without waiting on software developers.
Edge AI AI that runs on edge devices (outside of cloud data centers), meaning the computation is done locally on devices like smartphones, IoT sensors, or laptops (What Is Edge AI? | NVIDIA Blog). “Edge” implies low latency and data staying on the device. Edge AI models are optimized to use less power and memory, allowing real-time AI in the field without constant internet connection. Smart cameras doing real-time video analytics on the device (e.g. a security camera detecting intruders or factory camera spotting defects without sending video to the cloud); mobile AI apps (a phone app that translates signs via the camera *offline*, or AR apps that overlay filters on faces in real time); IoT devices (a sensor that uses AI to detect equipment failure signs on an oil rig, sending only alerts rather than raw data); autonomous drones/vehicles (onboard AI for navigation and obstacle detection without needing remote servers).
AI Ethics (Responsible AI) Principles and practices to ensure AI systems are developed and used in an ethical, fair, and transparent manner (Built-in Responsible AI | Feedzai). This includes addressing issues like bias (avoiding unfair discrimination), transparency (being able to explain AI decisions), privacy, and accountability for AI outcomes. Responsible AI governance helps maintain trust and comply with laws/regulations. Bias mitigation (e.g. checking that a loan approval AI isn’t biased against certain groups and adjusting it if it is); explainability tools (providing explanations for an AI’s decision in credit scoring or medical diagnosis for accountability); setting up an AI ethics committee or guidelines for an organization’s AI projects; ensuring compliance with regulations like the EU’s upcoming AI Act or industry guidelines by auditing AI systems. In sales conversations, clients might ask if an AI solution is “fair” or how it handles sensitive data—these fall under AI ethics.
Explainable AI (XAI) Techniques and methods that make the decisions of an AI model interpretable by humans (What Is Explainable AI? | Built In). In other words, XAI tries to explain why the AI produced a given output. This could be through visualizations (e.g. heatmaps on an image to show what the model “looked at”) or natural language explanations. Explainability is crucial for building trust, especially with complex “black box” models. Regulated industries like finance or healthcare where you need to justify decisions (e.g. an AI system that must explain why it denied an insurance claim or flagged a transaction as fraud); debugging models (developers use XAI tools to understand model errors or biases); customer trust in AI features (a bank might provide an explanation for an AI-determined credit score to a customer). Overall, XAI is applied whenever human oversight is needed on AI decisions – turning opaque predictions into something understandable.
